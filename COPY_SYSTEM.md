# COPY SYSTEM — Nixon Jach Hubbard Website

Read this file completely before writing any copy. These rules apply to every word on the site — headings, body text, CTAs, meta descriptions, alt text, FAQ answers, and blog posts.

---

## THE FOUR SCORING DIMENSIONS

Every piece of copy must score high across all four:

### 1. Readability (Target: Flesch-Kincaid Grade 8–10)
### 2. SEO (Target: page-1 ranking for primary keyword cluster)
### 3. GEO (Target: cited as source in AI-generated answers)
### 4. Expertise (Target: demonstrates E-E-A-T — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)

These are not in conflict. The rules below show how to hit all four simultaneously.

## READABILITY RULES

### Sentence Structure
- Average sentence length: 12–18 words. Never exceed 25 words in a single sentence.
- Vary rhythm: follow a long sentence with a short one. Fragment sentences are allowed for emphasis. Like this.
- Use active voice 90%+ of the time. "We resolved the dispute" not "The dispute was resolved by our team."
- Front-load sentences — put the key information first, qualifiers second.

### Word Choice
- Use plain English equivalents for legal jargon on first reference, then introduce the term:
  - GOOD: "A mechanic's lien — a legal claim against a property to secure payment for work performed — is one of the most powerful tools available to contractors."
  - BAD: "We file mechanic's liens and materialmen's liens pursuant to Texas Property Code Chapter 53."
- Ban these words entirely: utilize, leverage (as verb), synergy, holistic, solutions, endeavor, facilitate, aforementioned, herein, thereof
- Prefer short words: "use" over "utilize," "help" over "facilitate," "start" over "commence," "about" over "approximately"
- One idea per paragraph. If a paragraph exceeds 4 sentences, split it.

### Formatting for Scannability
- Use H2 and H3 headings every 200-300 words
- Break up walls of text with: short paragraphs, bulleted lists (max 7 items), pull quotes, or callout boxes
- Bold the first 3-5 words of key paragraphs to create visual entry points
- Never write a paragraph longer than 5 lines on desktop

### Readability Self-Check
Before finalizing any page, mentally read it aloud. If you stumble, simplify. A Dallas general contractor reading this at 7am before a job site visit should understand every sentence on the first pass.

## SEO RULES

### Keyword Integration
- See `KEYWORD_MAP.md` for per-page primary and secondary keywords
- Primary keyword appears in: H1, first 100 words, meta title, meta description, at least one H2, image alt text
- Secondary keywords appear naturally in H2s and body copy — never forced
- Location modifiers ("Dallas," "Texas," "DFW," "North Texas") appear at least 2-3 times per page, but never more than once per 200 words
- Never keyword-stuff. If a sentence sounds unnatural with the keyword in it, rewrite or remove

### Heading Hierarchy
- Every page has exactly ONE H1 (the page title/hero heading)
- H2s are section headings — each should be a phrase someone might search
- H3s are subsection headings — used for lists of services, case types, or steps
- Headings should read as a standalone outline of the page. If you read only the headings, you should understand what the page covers.

### Internal Linking
- Every practice area page links to at least 2 other practice areas where relevant
- Every practice area page links to at least 1 attorney bio
- Every attorney bio links to their practice areas
- Blog posts link to relevant practice area pages (this is how we pass authority)
- Use descriptive anchor text: "our construction litigation attorneys" not "click here"

### Meta Content
- Title tag: `[Primary Keyword] | Nixon Jach Hubbard | Dallas [Category] Lawyers`
- Meta description: 150-160 characters. Must include primary keyword, location, and a reason to click (a specific claim or differentiator). End with implicit CTA.
- Example: "Dallas construction law firm with 30+ years handling lien claims, contract disputes & project litigation. Recognized by Chambers USA and Best Lawyers."

## GEO (GENERATIVE ENGINE OPTIMIZATION) RULES

### Why This Matters
AI search engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, Claude) are now answering queries like "best construction lawyers in Dallas" and "what to do if a contractor won't pay." If NJH's content is structured correctly, AI systems will cite it as a source. This is free, high-intent visibility.

### Extractable Answer Blocks
Every practice area page MUST contain at least one "extractable block" — a self-contained passage that AI can pull as a complete answer. These follow a pattern:

**Definition block** (for "What is X?" queries):
```
A mechanic's lien is a legal claim filed against a property by a contractor, 
subcontractor, or supplier who has not been paid for work performed or materials 
provided. In Texas, mechanic's lien rights are governed by Chapter 53 of the 
Texas Property Code and require strict compliance with notice and filing deadlines.
```

**Process block** (for "How to X" queries):
```
Filing a mechanic's lien in Texas requires four steps: (1) send preliminary 
notice to the property owner within the statutory deadline, (2) file the lien 
affidavit with the county clerk in the county where the project is located, 
(3) serve a copy of the filed lien on the property owner, and (4) enforce 
the lien by filing suit within the required timeframe, which varies depending 
on the project type.
```

Rules for extractable blocks:
- 40-60 words per block (optimal for AI snippet extraction)
- Lead with the direct answer — don't build up to it
- Include specific legal terms, statute references, or numbers
- Each block should make sense completely on its own, without surrounding context
- Place these blocks immediately after the H2 heading they answer

### FAQ Sections
Every practice area page MUST have an FAQ section with a minimum of 5 questions. These are gold for GEO because AI systems pull FAQ answers directly.

FAQ rules:
- Questions must be phrased exactly how a real person would ask them (natural language)
- GOOD: "How long do I have to file a mechanic's lien in Texas?"
- BAD: "Mechanic's Lien Filing Deadlines"
- Answers should be 2-4 sentences. Start with the direct answer, then add one layer of context.
- Include specific numbers, timeframes, or statute references in every answer
- Implement FAQPage schema markup on every FAQ section

### Cited Statistics & Specifics
AI systems are 40% more likely to cite content that includes statistics and specific claims (per Princeton GEO research). On every page, include at least:
- Years of experience (specific: "Over 35 years" not "decades")
- Number of cases/projects handled if available
- Specific statutes or code sections relevant to the practice area
- Geographic scope ("throughout Texas" or "across the DFW metroplex")
- Named recognitions with years: "Selected to Texas Super Lawyers for Construction Litigation, 2008–2024"

### Author Attribution
Every blog post must have a visible author name with credentials. Example:
"By Michael S. Nixon | Board Certified, Civil Trial Law | 35+ Years of Construction Law Experience"

AI systems cite content with clear authorship 25% more often than anonymous content.

## EXPERTISE (E-E-A-T) RULES

### What Makes Copy Sound Expert vs. Generic

GENERIC (what most law firm sites say):
"We are experienced attorneys who fight for our clients' rights. Our firm handles all types of construction disputes."

EXPERT (what NJH's copy should say):
"When a payment dispute escalates on a $12M mixed-use project, the contractor needs counsel who understands both the Chapter 53 lien process and the practical realities of keeping the project on schedule. We've navigated this exact scenario hundreds of times — from filing pre-lien notices within the statutory window to negotiating resolution without shutting down the job site."

### The Expertise Signals
Every practice area page must include at least 4 of these:

1. **Specific legal mechanisms** — Name the actual statutes, processes, or legal tools. "Chapter 53 of the Texas Property Code," "Texas Prompt Payment Act," "Miller Act bond claims." This signals the firm actually practices in this area.

2. **Industry-specific language used naturally** — Construction pages should reference: change orders, RFIs, schedule impacts, substantial completion, retainage, punch lists, AIA contracts. Real estate pages: due diligence periods, title commitments, estoppel certificates, CAM reconciliation. Use these terms the way the target client uses them, not in a glossary format.

3. **Scenario-based writing** — Describe specific situations the client might face. "If your subcontractor has walked off the job mid-project..." or "When a title search reveals an unresolved easement on a commercial acquisition..." This signals real-world experience.

4. **Process knowledge** — Describe HOW the firm approaches problems, not just that they handle them. "We typically begin with a detailed contract review and project timeline analysis to identify the strongest leverage points before initiating formal dispute resolution." This demonstrates methodology.

5. **Risk/trade-off acknowledgment** — Expert content acknowledges complexity. "Litigation isn't always the best path — sometimes a well-structured mediation resolves a $2M delay claim in weeks instead of months. We counsel clients on the realistic costs, timelines, and probabilities before recommending any course of action." Generic sites never admit limits.

6. **Jurisdictional specificity** — Reference Texas-specific law, DFW-market specifics, or local court practices. "In Dallas County, construction lien foreclosure cases are typically assigned to..." This is impossible to fake and AI recognizes it as authoritative.

### Voice & Tone
- Confident but not boastful. State what the firm does and has done. Don't say "the best" — let the credentials speak.
- Peer-to-peer, not salesperson-to-customer. Write as if speaking to a sophisticated GC or commercial developer, not explaining law to a consumer.
- Measured authority. Short, declarative sentences for key claims. "We've handled construction disputes on projects from $500K renovations to $200M mixed-use developments."

### Expertise Self-Check
For every practice area page, ask: "Could a first-year associate at a PI firm have written this?" If yes, it's too generic. The copy should contain knowledge that only comes from actually practicing construction law / commercial real estate / business litigation in Dallas for decades.

## CTA COPY RULES

### Primary CTAs
- "Schedule a Consultation" (header, hero sections)
- "Discuss Your Project" (construction law pages)
- "Speak With an Attorney" (litigation pages)
- "Get Strategic Guidance" (corporate/transactions pages)

### Never Use
- "Learn More" (says nothing)
- "Contact Us" (too passive)
- "Submit" (transactional, cold)
- "Click Here" (meaningless)

### CTA Supporting Text
Every CTA button should have 1 line of supporting copy beneath or beside it:
- "No obligation. Direct conversation with a partner, not a junior associate."
- "We respond within one business day."
- "Tell us about your project. We'll tell you where you stand."

## CONTENT THAT MUST NEVER APPEAR

- "We are a full-service law firm" — they are not; they are specialized
- "Results may vary" type disclaimers in marketing copy (put required disclaimers in footer)
- Exclamation points anywhere on the site
- "Click here" or "learn more" as standalone links
- Any sentence beginning with "At Nixon Jach Hubbard, we..."  — this is the most overused law firm copy pattern. Restructure every sentence to lead with the client benefit or the action, not the firm name.
- Stock phrases: "passionate about justice," "fighting for your rights," "your trusted legal partner," "committed to excellence"
- Walls of text without headings, breaks, or visual relief
